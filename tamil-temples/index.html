UNESCO World Heritage Temples

Tamil Nadu is home to some of India's most significant UNESCO World Heritage Sites

1984

Group of Monuments at Mahabalipuram

Founded by the Pallava kings, these rathas (temples in the form of chariots), mandapas (cave sanctuaries), giant open-air rock reliefs such as the Descent of the Ganges, and the Shore Temple with thousands of sculptures to the glory of Shiva, constitute the most magnificent achievement of Pallava art.

1987

Brihadeeswarar Temple, Thanjavur

The Great Living Chola Temple was first inscribed in 1987. Built by Raja Raja Chola I between 1003 and 1010 AD, this temple is a brilliant testimony to the heights achieved by the Cholas in temple architecture. The vimana tower stands 66 meters tall, one of the tallest in the world.

2004

Gangaikonda Cholapuram & Airavatesvara Temple

The Great Living Chola Temples designation was extended to include these two masterpieces. Together, the three temples demonstrate a progressive development of high Chola architecture and represent the pinnacle of Tamil cultural achievement.

Five sacred Shiva temples representing the five prime elements of nature - Earth, Water, Fire, Air, and Ether

Kanchipuram

Srikalahasti Temple

Srikalahasti, Andhra Pradesh

Ether (Akasha)

Thillai Nataraja Temple

Chidambaram

Houses the Akasha Lingam. Unlike other temples, Shiva is represented as Nataraja (cosmic dancer). Features the famous Chidambara Rahasyam (secret of Chidambaram).

Remarkably, all five Pancha Bootha Stalam temples are geographically aligned in almost a straight line across South India. This positioning is astonishing as the temples were built centuries ago without modern surveying technology. The presiding deities are revered in the 7th century Tevaram, composed by the Nayanar saint poets.

108 Divya Desam

The 108 sacred abodes of Lord Vishnu, glorified in the hymns of the twelve Alvar saint-poets

What are Divya Desams?

The 108 Divya Desam ("Divine Abodes") are sacred Vishnu temples revered in the Naalayira Divya Prabandham, a collection of 4,000 Tamil verses composed by the twelve Alvars between the 5th and 8th centuries CE. In Sanskrit, "divya" means divine and "desam" indicates realm or temple.

The Alvars

The twelve Alvar saint-poets were instrumental in popularising Vaishnavism during the early medieval period. Their devotional hymns (pasurams) describing these temples form the foundation of Sri Vaishnava tradition. The temples mentioned in their songs are classified as Divya Desams.

Distribution

Of the 108 temples, 84 are in Tamil Nadu, 11 in Kerala, 4 in Uttar Pradesh, 3 in Uttarakhand, 2 in Andhra Pradesh, 1 in Gujarat, 1 in Nepal, and 2 are believed to be in the celestial realm (Tirupparkadal and Vaikuntham).

Postures of Vishnu

Lord Vishnu appears in three sacred postures across the 108 temples: Standing (Nindra Thirukkolam) in 60 temples, Sleeping (Kidantha Thirukkolam) in 27 temples, and Sitting (Veetrirundha Thirukkolam) in 21 temples.

Pilgrimage Travel Tips

Tamil Nadu (84 temples)

Plan 12 days minimum. Start from Srirangam (Trichy) and move through Thanjavur, Kumbakonam, Mayiladuthurai, Sirkazhi, Cuddalore, Kanchipuram, Chennai, Madurai, and Tirunelveli.

Kerala (11 temples)

Plan 3 days. Start from Thiruvananthapuram, move through Chengannur (6 temples in one day), then to Ernakulam, and finally Thrissur region.

North India & Nepal

Andhra Pradesh (2-3 days), Gujarat (1 day), Uttar Pradesh (4 days), Uttarakhand (4 days), Nepal (8 days). Plan separately based on season.

General Advice

After visiting a temple, ask the priest for directions to the next Divya Desam. Carry Indian currency in small denominations. Best visiting hours: 7 AM to 12 PM and 4 PM to 7 PM.

About Tamil Nadu Temples

The temple land of India

Tamil Nadu is often called the "Land of Temples" and rightly so. The state is home to over 33,000 ancient temples, many of which are over a thousand years old. These temples are not just places of worship but living museums of art, architecture, and cultural heritage.

The temple-building tradition in Tamil Nadu spans over two millennia, from the Sangam period to the Pallava, Chola, Pandya, and Nayak dynasties. Each dynasty left its distinctive architectural mark, creating a rich tapestry of styles from the rock-cut caves of the Pallavas to the towering gopurams of the Nayaks.

The Chola dynasty (9th-13th century CE) is widely regarded as the golden age of Tamil temple architecture. The Brihadeeswarar Temple in Thanjavur, built by Raja Raja Chola I, remains one of the most magnificent temples ever built, with its 66-meter vimana that was the tallest structure in India for centuries.

These temples are also the birthplace of the Bhakti movement, where saint-poets like the Nayanars (Shaivite) and Alvars (Vaishnavite) composed devotional hymns that continue to be sung today, preserving Tamil literary heritage spanning 1,500 years.

Quick Facts

  • Oldest Temple: Saluvankuppam Murugan Temple (c. 300 BCE)
  • Largest Temple: Sri Ranganathaswamy, Srirangam (156 acres)
  • Tallest Gopuram: Murdeshwar Temple Rajagopuram (72m)
  • Tallest Vimana: Brihadeeswarar Temple (66m)
  • Major Dynasties: Pallava, Chola, Pandya, Nayak, Vijayanagara
  • UNESCO Sites: 4 temple-related World Heritage Sites
  • Paadal Petra Sthalam: 276 Shiva temples glorified by Nayanars
